Week 4

Cognitivism

Overview

Cognitivism is a learning theory that emphasizes the role of mental processes in learning. According to cognitivist theory, learning involves the acquisition and organization of knowledge in the mind, which can be facilitated through various instructional methods such as scaffolding, guided practice, and feedback.
Cognitivism also emphasizes the importance of metacognition and self-regulated learning, which involves thinking about one's own thinking and using strategies to monitor and control learning processes. Overall, cognitivism provides a useful framework for understanding how learners acquire, process, and use information to develop knowledge and skills.

Jean Piaget (1896-1980)

Piaget's work has been influential in both constructivist and cognitive theories of learning. Piaget believed that cognitive development happens in stages and that children actively construct their own understanding of the world through interactions with it. Piaget's ideas have been especially influential in educational theory, where they have been used to develop curricula for learners at various stages of cognitive development.

Jerome Bruner (1915-2016)

Bruner was an American psychologist who developed the concept of the "scaffolding" of learning. He argued that learners need support and guidance from more knowledgeable others in order to develop their cognitive abilities. Bruner emphasized the importance of active, problem-solving learning experiences that help learners construct their own understanding of the world.

Benjamin Bloom (1913-1999)

He was an American educational psychologist who made significant contributions to the development of cognitivism. Bloom is best known for his work on Bloom's Taxonomy, which is a hierarchical framework for categorizing educational goals and objectives. Bloom's Taxonomy consists of six levels of learning objectives, ranging from lower-order thinking skills to higher-order thinking skills. In addition to his work on Bloom's Taxonomy, Bloom also conducted research on the nature of learning and the factors that influence learning outcomes. Bloom's work has had a significant impact on educational theory and practice, particularly in the development of curricula and instructional materials that promote higher-order thinking skills and mastery learning.

Strenghts and Limitations

Cognitivism has several strengths and limitations when it comes to learning a new language.

  • Emphasis on mental processes and knowledge acquisition aligns well with language learning, which involves understanding and using complex linguistic structures.
  • Emphasis on the importance of practice and feedback aligns well with language learning, which requires regular opportunities for speaking, writing, and receiving feedback.
  • Focus on metacognition (thinking about one's own thinking) can help learners to develop awareness of their own language learning processes, which can lead to more effective learning.
  • Emphasis on individual mental processes may not fully account for the social and cultural aspects of language learning, such as the importance of interaction with native speakers and immersion in the language.
  • Cognitive overload can occur when learners are presented with too much information or complex tasks, which can hinder learning.
  • Cognitivist approaches may not account for the affective factors that can impact language learning, such as motivation, anxiety, and self-efficacy.

Implication in Instructional Design

Cognitivism has several implications for instructional design, including the importance of aligning materials with learners' prior knowledge, providing opportunities for practice and feedback, creating a supportive learning environment, and assessing learning outcomes. Overall, cognitivist approaches provide a useful framework for designing effective instructional materials and activities that promote meaningful learning.
