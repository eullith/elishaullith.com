Week 3

Constructivism

Overview

Constructivism is a learning theory that emphasizes the active role of learners in constructing their own understanding of the world. This approach assumes that knowledge is not simply transferred from a teacher or textbook to the learner, but rather, constructed based on the individual's experiences and interactions with the environment. In this sense, constructivism places the learner at the center of the learning process.
Constructivist learning experiences are characterized by active exploration, inquiry, problem-solving, and collaboration. The learner is encouraged to construct meaning by building on their existing knowledge, connecting new information to prior experiences, and making sense of new ideas in light of their own perspectives and values.

Jean Piaget (1896-1980)

Piaget was a Swiss psychologist who is best known for his theory of cognitive development. According to Piaget, children construct knowledge through their interactions with the physical world, and their thinking develops in a series of stages. Piaget's ideas had a significant influence on constructivist theory, particularly in the emphasis on learners actively constructing their own understanding of the world.

Lev Vygotsky (1896-1934)

Vygotsky was a Soviet psychologist who developed the sociocultural theory of learning. According to Vygotsky, learning is a social and cultural activity that occurs through interactions with others. Vygotsky emphasized the role of language in learning and believed that learners construct knowledge through dialogue and collaboration with others. His ideas have had a significant impact on constructivist theory, particularly in the emphasis on social interaction and collaboration.

Ernst von Glasersfeld (1917-2010)

Ernst von Glasersfeld was an Austrian philosopher who is best known for his work on radical constructivism. He emphasized the importance of personal and subjective meaning-making in learning and believed that learners construct knowledge through their own experiences and interactions with the world. von Glasersfeld's ideas have been influential in constructivist theory, particularly in the emphasis on learners actively constructing their own understanding of the world.

Strenghts and Limitations

Constructivism can help language learners by emphasizing the importance of context, interaction, and collaboration. However, it can also present challenges.

  • Prioritizes collaboration and social interaction, which can help learners practice using the language in authentic situations
  • Can be particularly effective for learners who are already motivated and interested in the language
  • Can help learners develop problem-solving and critical thinking skills
  • May require significant scaffolding and support, particularly for beginners who are not familiar with the language and cultural norms
  • Can be time-consuming, as it requires learners to actively engage with the language and construct meaning in a way that is often more complex than simply memorizing information
  • May not be effective for all learners, particularly those who are not comfortable with group work or who prefer a more individualized approach to learning.

Implication in Instructional Design

Instructional design can benefit from a constructivist approach by promoting active and collaborative learning experiences that build on learners' existing knowledge and experiences.
Network-based language teaching and cooperative learning are examples of instructional strategies that incorporate constructivist principles.
