Week 2

Behaviorism

Overview

Behaviorism is based on the idea that all behavior is learned through interactions with the environment. In other words, behaviorists believe that the way we act is a result of the experiences we have had. (Cherry, 2022)
In essence, behaviorism is a means of comprehending how our experiences affect the way we behave and the ways in which we may modify our behavior via rewards and punishments. It emphasizes behavior analysis and observation of how controlled environmental changes impact it. If a behavior is followed by a reward, it's more likely to be repeated. If a behavior is followed by a punishment, it's less likely to be repeated.

Pavlov studied a form of learning behavior called a conditioned reflex, in which an animal or human produced a reflex (unconscious) response to a stimulus and, over time, was conditioned to produce the response to a different stimulus that the experimenter associated with the original stimulus.

The reflex Pavlov worked with was salivation in response to the presence of food. The salivation reflex could be elicited using a second stimulus, such as a specific sound, that was presented in association with the initial food stimulus several times. Once the response to the second stimulus was “learned,” the food stimulus could be omitted. Pavlov’s “classical conditioning” is only one form of learning behavior studied by behaviorists.

John B. Watson was an influential American psychologist in the early twentieth century who believed that the study of consciousness was flawed and instead focused on observable behavior. He proposed a shift in the focus of psychology from the mind to behavior, which came to be known as behaviorism. Behaviorists studied learned behavior and its interaction with inborn qualities of the organism, commonly using animals in experiments. Tolman believed that everything important in psychology could be investigated through the continued experimental and theoretical analysis of the determiners of rat behavior.

Behaviorism dominated experimental psychology for several decades and has had a lasting influence. It established psychology as a scientific discipline through objective methods and experimentation and is still used in therapy and behavior modification. Behaviorism has also led to research on environmental influences on human behavior and is commonly used in classroom settings.

B.F. Skinner was a psychologist who studied how behavior is affected by the things that happen after it. He believed that rewards and punishments were very important in making us behave a certain way. Skinner made a special box called a Skinner box, which helps researchers study behavior. The box isolates the animal being studied and has a lever or button that the animal can push. When the animal pushes the lever or button, the box can give a reward like food or a punishment like a loud noise. This helps researchers understand how behavior can be changed by rewards and punishments.

Skinner was a psychologist who focused on how positive and negative reinforcement can influence behavior. This idea has had a big impact on psychology, but isn't used as much today with new research in cognitive psychology. However, people still use this method to change behavior. Skinner also wrote books about how his approach could make people happier, but not everyone agrees with his ideas.

Strenghts and Limitations

Although behaviorism can be useful in the education of caregivers, it does have some limitations.

  • Behaviorism emphasizes the importance of reinforcement and punishment in shaping behavior. Caregivers can use this principle to encourage desired behaviors, such as taking medication on time or following a specific diet.
  • Behavior therapy, which is based on the principles of behaviorism, has been used to treat a variety of psychological disorders that may be present in elderly people, such as depression and anxiety.
  • Behaviorism can help caregivers break down complex tasks into smaller, more manageable steps, which can make it easier for elderly people to learn new skills or routines.
  • Behaviorism does not take into account internal mental processes or individual differences in behavior. Elderly people may have unique needs and preferences that cannot be fully addressed through a behaviorist approach.
  • Behaviorism can be overly focused on external reinforcement and punishment, which may not always be effective or appropriate for elderly people who may have cognitive or physical impairments.
  • Behaviorism may not be well-suited to address the emotional and social needs of elderly people, which are important for their overall well-being.
While behaviorism can be a useful approach in the education of caregivers, it is necessary to acknowledge its limitations and use it in ways that align with other approaches that take into account the unique needs and preferences of individual senior citizens.

Implication in Instructional Design

The implications of behaviorism for instructional or learning design are significant. In essence, it means creating effective learning experiences that includes behaviorist principles. One important implication is that complex learning tasks must be broken down into smaller, more digestible pieces. This can help learners increment their knowledge and skills, maximizing their chances of success. Another implication is the importance of providing both positive and negative feedback to help learners understand their progress and areas for improvement. Furthermore, behaviorism focuses on the value of implementing a number of interactive techniques, such as quizzes, simulations, and role-playing, to engage and reinforce learners. Finally, behaviorism highlights the value of ensuring that students can leverage their newly acquired knowledge in new and diverse situations.
