Week 2

Behaviorism

Overview

Behaviorism is based on the idea that all behavior is learned through interactions with the environment. In other words, behaviorists believe that the way we act is a result of the experiences we have had. (Cherry, 2022)
In essence, behaviorism is a means of comprehending how our experiences affect the way we behave and the ways in which we may modify our behavior via rewards and punishments. It emphasizes behavior analysis and observation of how controlled environmental changes impact it. If a behavior is followed by a reward, it's more likely to be repeated. If a behavior is followed by a punishment, it's less likely to be repeated.
The development of behaviorism in learning is generally associated with a number of key dates and events in history of psychology.
To learn more about the key researchers and important dates and milestones in the development of behaviorism, simply click on each tab.

In 1913, John B. Watson published a landmark article in the Psychology Review, the "Psychology as the Behaviorist View". In this article Watson laid out basic principles of behaviorism and argued why psychology should focus on observable, measurable behaviour rather than subjective experiences like thoughts and emotions.

The publication of Watson's book "Psychology from the Standpoint of a Behaviorist," which outlines his ideas about behaviorism and the role of environment in shaping behavior.

The establishment of the Society for the Experimental Analysis of Behavior, which is still an important organization for behaviorist researchers and practitioners.

The publication of B.F. Skinner's book "The Behavior of Organisms," which introduced the concept of operant conditioning and emphasized the importance of reinforcement in shaping behavior.

The publication of Skinner's book "Science and Human Behavior," which applied principles of behaviorism to social and cultural issues.
